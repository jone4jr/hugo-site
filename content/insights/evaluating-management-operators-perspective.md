After 15+ years managing operations and analyzing hundreds of management teams as an investor, I've learned that most investment research misses what actually matters when evaluating executives.

Analysts focus on earnings calls and presentations. I focus on operational execution. Here's what I look for.

## The Metrics That Actually Matter

### 1. Working Capital Management

Show me how management handles working capital, and I'll show you how they actually run the business.

**Green flags:**
- Days inventory trending down while service levels maintain
- DSO (days sales outstanding) stable or improving
- DPO (days payable outstanding) extended without supplier issues
- Seasonal working capital swings well-managed

**Red flags:**
- Inventory builds without revenue growth
- AR aging deteriorating
- Supplier payment terms tightening  
- Working capital consuming cash while revenue grows

Management teams that optimize working capital understand their business intimately. Those who don't... don't.

### 2. Capital Allocation Track Record

Words are cheap. Look at what they've actually done with cash over the last 5-10 years.

**Questions to answer:**
- Did acquisitions create value or destroy it?
- Were share buybacks executed opportunistically or poorly timed?
- Has capex generated returns above cost of capital?
- Do they return excess cash or hoard it?
- Have they managed debt prudently?

One great management team I studied:
- Bought back 30% of shares during 2020 crash (average $12/share)
- Sold non-core division at 14x EBITDA (market valued core at 8x)
- Invested in automation that improved margins 400bps
- Stock now at $32

They earned trust through execution, not presentations.

### 3. Operational Metrics Trajectory

Forget the narrative. Watch the numbers.

**Leading indicators of good operators:**

*Margin expansion from operational improvement*
- Not pricing alone
- Not just cost cuts
- Sustainable efficiency gains

*Inventory turns improving*
- Better demand planning
- Tighter production scheduling
- Stronger supplier relationships

*Quality metrics trending right*
- Lower scrap rates
- Reduced warranty claims
- Higher first-pass yield

*Employee metrics*
- Stable/improving retention
- Lower turnover in key positions
- Productive workforce growth

These don't lie. Spreadsheets and presentations do.

## What Earnings Calls Actually Tell You

Most investors listen for guidance and Q&A. I listen for how management thinks.

### Good Operators Sound Like:

**Specific and Detailed**
> "We're targeting 200 basis points of margin expansion through three initiatives: automation of our finishing process (120bps), raw material optimization (50bps), and energy efficiency improvements (30bps). The finishing automation requires $3M capex with 18-month payback."

They know their numbers. They know the drivers. They can articulate the plan.

**Realistic About Challenges**
> "Q2 was tough. We had three customer audits that diverted management attention, a supplier quality issue that cost us two weeks of production, and our new ERP system had integration problems. We learned from it. Here's what we're changing..."

Bad quarters happen. Good management owns them and adjusts.

**Focused on Long-Term**
> "We could hit quarterly numbers by cutting training budget and deferring maintenance. We're not doing that. We're investing in our people and equipment because that's how you build a business that lasts."

Short-term sacrifice for long-term strength.

### Bad Operators Sound Like:

**Vague and Hand-Wavy**
> "We're implementing numerous strategic initiatives across multiple platforms to drive operational excellence and stakeholder value creation through our integrated ecosystem approach."

Translation: We have no idea what we're doing.

**Blame External Factors**
> "Results were impacted by headwinds, macro uncertainty, and unprecedented market conditions..."

Good operators figure it out. Poor ones make excuses.

**Over-Promise**
> "We expect significant margin expansion, substantial revenue growth, and transformational improvements ahead."

Show me the plan, not the adjectives.

## The Site Visit Advantage

When possible, visit facilities. You learn more in 4 hours on-site than 4 years of earnings calls.

**What I look for:**

*Organization and cleanliness*
- 5S implemented (visual management visible)?
- Tools and materials organized?
- Safety protocols obvious?

A messy shop floor = messy operations = messy results.

*Employee engagement*
- Do people know their metrics?
- Can operators explain the process?
- Is there visible pride in work?

Engaged workforce = good leadership.

*Asset condition*
- Well-maintained equipment?
- Preventive maintenance schedules visible?
- Modern vs. obsolete technology?

How they treat assets reveals how they think.

*Data visibility*
- Metrics posted and current?
- Quality data tracked in real-time?
- Production boards updated?

Data-driven or gut-feel?

## The Ultimate Test: Crisis Response

How management handles adversity reveals everything.

**2020 Example: Two Companies, Same Industry**

**Company A Management:**
- Furloughed 40% of workforce in March
- Cut all capex
- Drew down entire credit line "just in case"
- Suspended dividend to "preserve cash"
- Ended year with $50M cash, zero debt, skeleton crew

Results: Lost market share, demoralized workforce, customers diversified suppliers.

**Company B Management:**
- Kept everyone employed, reduced hours
- Accelerated strategic capex (automation)
- Worked with lenders to add covenant flexibility
- Maintained dividend
- Used downturn to upgrade facilities

Results: Gained market share, stronger customer relationships, improved competitive position.

Guess which company I invested in?

## Red Flags That Matter

Some things are automatic disqualifiers:

**Serial Acquirers Without Integration Discipline**

If every earnings call features a new acquisition but organic growth stagnates, they're masking operational weakness with deal flow.

**Frequent Reorganizations**

One restructuring = addressing problems. Three restructuring in five years = management doesn't know what they're doing.

**Compensation Misaligned With Shareholders**

When executives get rich while shareholders get poor, that tells you everything.

**Lack of Skin in the Game**

If management owns minimal stock (not options, actual stock), their interests aren't aligned with yours.

## The Bottom Line

Good management is hard to spot from afar, but these principles help:

1. **Track record matters more than credentials**
2. **Operational metrics reveal more than presentations**
3. **Capital allocation is the ultimate test**
4. **Crisis response shows true colors**
5. **Actions speak louder than words**

As an investor with operational experience, my edge is knowing the difference between a great operator and a great talker.

The market often confuses the two. That's where opportunity lives.
