Change fatigue often comes from whiplash, not change itself.

People can handle hard adjustments when the frame is clear. They struggle when every message feels like a reset, every priority sounds urgent, and nobody can tell what is still true from last week.

## Name what is changing

Be concrete. What process, role, schedule, standard, system, or decision is changing? When does it change? Who is affected?

Avoid vague language like "we are transforming" when people need to know what they will do differently Monday morning.

## Name what is staying the same

Stability matters during change. Tell the team which standards, priorities, values, or routines remain in place.

That reduces the sense that everything is up for grabs.

## Explain the reason without overselling

People do not need a perfect story. They need an honest one.

Explain the business reason, customer need, safety issue, cost pressure, or learning that made change necessary. Avoid pretending there will be no friction.

## Create adoption ownership

Project ownership gets the change launched. Adoption ownership keeps it alive.

Name who will check whether the new behavior is happening, how blockers will be raised, and when the team will review what is working.

Change feels less exhausting when leaders make the next step visible and keep enough stability for people to move with confidence.
